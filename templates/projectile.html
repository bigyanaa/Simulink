Projectile Motion

Projectile motion combines constant horizontal velocity with vertical acceleration due to gravity, resulting in a parabolic trajectory.

Key Equations

Horizontal Motion

$$ x = v_0\cos(\theta)t $$

$$ v_x = v_0\cos(\theta) $$

$$ a_x = 0 $$

Vertical Motion

$$ y = v_0\sin(\theta)t - \frac{1}{2}gt^2 $$

$$ v_y = v_0\sin(\theta) - gt $$

$$ a_y = -g $$

Important Parameters

Maximum Height

$$ h_{max} = \frac{v_0^2\sin^2(\theta)}{2g} $$

Time of Flight

$$ T = \frac{2v_0\sin(\theta)}{g} $$

Range

$$ R = \frac{v_0^2\sin(2\theta)}{g} $$

Maximum range occurs at θ = 45°

Review Questions

  1. Question: A ball is thrown with an initial velocity of 20 m/s at an angle of 30° above the horizontal. Calculate its maximum height.

    Answer: Using h = (v₀²sin²θ)/(2g) = (20²×sin²30°)/(2×9.81) = 5.1 meters

  2. Question: Why is 45° the angle for maximum range in projectile motion?

    Answer: At 45°, there's an optimal balance between horizontal and vertical components of velocity. This maximizes the range equation R = (v₀²sin2θ)/g, as sin(2×45°) = sin(90°) = 1

  3. Question: A projectile is launched at 60° and 30°. Compare the ranges.

    Answer: The ranges will be equal because sin(2×60°) = sin(2×30°) = sin(120°) = sin(60°) = 0.866. This demonstrates the complementary angle property in projectile motion.

  4. Question: How does air resistance affect projectile motion in reality?

    Answer: Air resistance reduces both the range and maximum height, makes the path asymmetric (steeper descent than ascent), and shifts the maximum height point closer to the launch point.
