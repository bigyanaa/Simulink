Pendulum Motion

A simple pendulum demonstrates simple harmonic motion, where the restoring force is proportional to displacement and acts in the opposite direction.

Key Equations

Period of Oscillation

$$ T = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{L}{g}} $$

  • T = Period
  • L = Length of pendulum
  • g = Gravitational acceleration (9.81 m/s²)
Angular Displacement

$$ \theta(t) = \theta_0\cos(\omega t + \phi) $$

  • θ₀ = Maximum angular displacement
  • ω = Angular frequency
  • φ = Phase constant

Energy Conservation

$$ E_{total} = KE + PE = constant $$

$$ \frac{1}{2}mv^2 + mgh = \frac{1}{2}mL^2\dot{\theta}^2 + mgL(1-\cos\theta) $$

  • At maximum displacement: All energy is potential
  • At equilibrium: All energy is kinetic
  • Total energy remains constant (ignoring air resistance)

Important Properties

  • Period is independent of mass
  • Period is independent of amplitude (for small angles)
  • Motion follows sinusoidal pattern
  • Small angle approximation: sin θ ≈ θ

Applications

Practical Uses

  • Grandfather Clocks
  • Seismographs
  • Metronomes
  • Structural Testing

Real-World Examples

  • Swing Sets
  • Building Stabilizers
  • Scientific Instruments
  • Time Measurement

Review Questions

  1. Question: A simple pendulum has a length of 2.0 meters. Calculate its period on Earth (g = 9.81 m/s²).

    Answer: Using T = 2π√(L/g) = 2π√(2/9.81) = 2.84 seconds

  2. Question: Why does a grandfather clock pendulum swing slower on a hot day?

    Answer: The metal rod expands with heat, increasing the pendulum's length. Since T ∝ √L, a longer pendulum has a longer period.

  3. Question: A pendulum's period doubles. By what factor did its length change?

    Answer: Since T ∝ √L, if T doubles, L must increase by a factor of 4 (quadruple).

  4. Question: Why are pendulum clocks less accurate at high altitudes?

    Answer: The gravitational field strength (g) decreases with altitude, affecting the period T = 2π√(L/g), making the pendulum swing slower.
